When people shop for a new furnace or AC, they usually ask about the brand first. It's a fair question — but it's not the one that decides whether you'll be happy with the system. Sizing is.

## What a load calculation actually is

A load calculation measures what your home actually needs to stay comfortable — based on its square footage, layout, insulation, windows, and how it's built. It's the difference between installing the right-size system for your house and installing whatever was there before. It takes a little more time up front, and it's worth it every day after.

## Why bigger isn't better

It's tempting to think a bigger unit is a safer bet. It isn't. An oversized system blasts on, hits temperature fast, and shuts off — over and over. That short-cycling leaves your house unevenly heated or cooled, does a poor job pulling humidity out of the air in summer, and wears the equipment out early. An undersized system has the opposite problem: it runs and runs and never quite gets there.

## The most common reason a new system disappoints

More often than not, when someone's unhappy with a brand-new system, the equipment is fine — it was just sized wrong. Get the sizing right and a mid-range system will quietly outperform a premium one that was guessed at. That's why we run a real load calculation before we quote anyone a new system, and why we'd rather do it right the first time than come back to explain a comfort problem later.

If you're planning a replacement, ask whoever's quoting you whether they're doing a load calculation. If the answer is "we'll just match the old one," keep asking.
